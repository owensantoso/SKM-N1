Overview

This chapter focuses on example-based expressions in Japanese, specifically the different ways of offering options, making generalizations, and emphasizing inclusivity in Japanese statements. These expressions vary in formality, usage, and nuance, but they share a common function: they illustrate patterns by providing examples or describing situations inclusively.

Each grammar point has specific contexts where it’s preferred, and there are subtle differences in how each point conveys choices, generality, or inclusiveness. Here's a brief overview of the four main grammar points in this chapter:

  1. ～なり…なり: Offers options or examples, implying that any of the suggested choices are fine.
  2. ～であれ…であれ / ～であろうと…であろうと: Expresses that a statement applies no matter which option or situation is chosen.
  3. ～といい…といい: Highlights specific qualities of a subject by pointing out two examples, usually with a positive or negative evaluation.
  4. ～といわず…といわず: Conveys comprehensive inclusivity by listing examples that cover all parts, aspects, or times of a subject without exception.

1. ～なり…なり

Meaning: "Either... or...; this or that"

Japanese: 「～でもいいし…でもいいから、何かをする」

Usage: Offers options or examples, implying that any of the suggested choices are fine. This structure is often used to suggest possible actions or choices in an informal or neutral context, but with a tone that is slightly urging.

Formality: Casual to semi-formal. Suitable for both spoken and written Japanese in casual to semi-formal settings.

Structure: 名詞 + なり / 動詞辞書形 + なり

Conjugation Examples:

  • 名詞 + なり: 映画えいがなり音楽おんがくなり、好きなものを選んでください。
    "Please choose what you like, either movies or music."
  • 動詞辞書形 + なり: 休みの日は、本ほんを読むなり、散歩さんぽするなりしてリラックスします。
    "On my days off, I relax by either reading books or taking walks."

Example Sentences:

  • 昼休みひるやすみは40分しかないんだから、おにぎりおにぎりなりサンドイッチサンドイッチなり何か買って早く食べたほうがいい。
    "Since lunch break is only 40 minutes, you should quickly buy something, like an onigiri or a sandwich, and eat."
  • お手伝いできることはいたしますよ。私になりオーエンオーエンなり言ってください。
    "If there's anything I can help with, please tell me or Owen."
  • 言葉の意味がわからなかったら、辞書で調べるなりインターネットで探すなりしてみたらどうですか。
    "If you don’t understand the meaning of a word, why not try looking it up in a dictionary or searching the internet?"
  • この魚、僕が釣ったんだ。焼くなり煮るなりして食べてみて。
    "I caught this fish. Try cooking it however you like, by grilling or boiling it."
  • オーエンオーエンは宿題をするなり、掃除をするなり、少しは何か手伝ってほしい。
    "I wish Owen would at least help out a little by either doing his homework or cleaning."

Comparison with Other Grammar Structures:

～なり…なり vs. ～であれ…であれ

～なり…なり is used to offer choices or suggest actions in a casual or conversational tone, while ～であれ…であれ is more formal and used to generalize across types or categories, emphasizing that the main point applies regardless of the specific option chosen.

  • ～なり…なり:
    "おにぎりなりサンドイッチなり何か買って早く食べたほうがいい。"
    (Suggests that buying either an onigiri or a sandwich is acceptable.)
  • ～であれ…であれ:
    "地震であれ火事であれ、緊急の場合に冷静になれる人は少ないだろう。"
    (Generalizes that few people can stay calm in any emergency, whether it's an earthquake or a fire.)

Simpler Version

This grammar point is a more formal and slightly urging way to offer options. In everyday conversation, people might use simpler structures like ～でもいいし…でもいい.

  • Original (～なり…なり):
    おにぎりおにぎりなりサンドイッチサンドイッチなり何か買って早く食べたほうがいい。
    "You should quickly buy something, like an onigiri or a sandwich, and eat."
  • Simpler Version:
    おにぎりおにぎりでもいいしサンドイッチサンドイッチでもいいから、何か買って早く食べたほうがいい。
    "It's okay to have an onigiri or a sandwich, so you should buy something and eat quickly."

Nuance Difference: The original ～なり…なり has a slightly urging tone, encouraging the listener to take action. The simpler version using ～でもいいし…でもいい is more neutral and commonly used in everyday speech.

Notes:

  • Avoid using past tense verbs after this structure.
  • Often used with sentences expressing the speaker's hope, intent, or suggestions.
  • Not typically used in highly formal or professional settings.

2. ～であれ…であれ / ～であろうと…であろうと

Meaning: "Whether... or...; Regardless of..."

Japanese: 「～でも…でも関係なく、同じ種類のものにはみんな同じことが言える」

Usage: Expresses inclusivity across different scenarios, implying that a certain statement applies no matter which option or situation is chosen. Used for making generalized statements.

Formality: Neutral to formal. Suitable for both spoken and written Japanese, particularly in formal contexts.

Structure: 名詞 + であれ / 名詞 + であろうと

Conjugation Examples:

  • 名詞 + であれ: 雨あめであれ雪ゆきであれ、彼は毎日ジョギングする。
    "Whether it's rain or snow, he jogs every day."
  • 名詞 + であろうと: 成功せいこうであろうと失敗しっぱいであろうと、全力を尽くすことが大切だ。
    "Whether it's success or failure, it's important to give it your all."

Example Sentences:

  • 地震じしんであれ火事かじであれ、緊急の場合に冷静になれる人は少ないだろう。
    "Whether it's an earthquake or a fire, there are few people who can stay calm in an emergency."
  • 文学ぶんがくであれ音楽おんがくであれ、芸術げいじゅつには才能さいのうが必要なのだ。
    "Whether it's literature or music, talent is essential for the arts."
  • 禁煙きんえんであれ禁酒きんしゅであれ、周りの人の協力が大切だと思う。
    "Whether it's quitting smoking or drinking, the support of people around you is crucial."
  • ビールビールであろうとワインワインであろうと、酒は酒だ。運転前に絶対飲んではいけない。
    "Whether it's beer or wine, alcohol is alcohol. You should never drink before driving."
  • オーエンオーエンであれ黄こうであれ、どちらでも勝つために最善を尽くすべきだ。
    "Whether it's Owen or Huang, either one should do their best to win."

Comparison with Other Grammar Structures:

～であれ…であれ vs. ～なり…なり

～なり…なり suggests choices and encourages action, while ～であれ…であれ generalizes across types or categories without implying choices.

  • ～なり…なり:
    "オーエンなり黄なり、誰かと相談してみて。"
    (Suggesting to consult with either Owen or Huang.)
  • ～であれ…であれ:
    "オーエンであれ黄であれ、責任を持って行動するべきだ。"
    (Emphasizing that both Owen and Huang should act responsibly.)

Simpler Version

This grammar point is a more formal way to express "whether... or...". In everyday conversation, people might use simpler structures like ～でも…でも.

  • Original (～であれ…であれ):
    地震じしんであれ火事かじであれ、緊急の場合に冷静になれる人は少ないだろう。
    "Whether it's an earthquake or a fire, there are few people who can stay calm in an emergency."
  • Simpler Version:
    地震じしんでも火事かじでも、緊急の場合に冷静になれる人は少ないだろう。
    "Whether it's an earthquake or a fire, there are few people who can stay calm in an emergency."

Nuance Difference: The original ～であれ…であれ has a more formal and generalized tone, suitable for written or formal speech. The simpler version using ～でも…でも is more commonly used in everyday conversation and sounds more natural in casual contexts.

Notes:

  • Used to make neutral generalizations.
  • Suitable for formal statements and written language.
  • Does not convey personal opinions; it's objective.

3. ～といい…といい

Meaning: "Both... and... (express admiration or criticism)"

Japanese: 「～を見ても…を見ても同じような状態だ」

Usage: Highlights specific qualities of a subject by pointing out two examples, usually with a positive or negative evaluation.

Formality: Semi-formal to casual. Used in both spoken and written Japanese, often in descriptive or evaluative contexts.

Structure: 名詞 + といい

Conjugation Examples:

  • 名詞 + といい: この料理りょうりといい見た目みためといい、最高だ。
    "This dish is excellent, both in taste and appearance."

Example Sentences:

  • この映画えいがといい映像えいぞうといい音楽おんがくといい、最高の作品だ。
    "This movie is the best work, with both its beautiful visuals and excellent music."
  • オーエンオーエンのスピーチは発音はつおんといい話し方はなしかたといい、完璧だった。
    "Owen's speech was perfect, both in pronunciation and delivery."
  • この虫むしといい色いろといい形かたちといい、木きの葉はにそっくりだ。
    "This insect resembles a leaf in both color and shape."
  • 黄こうといい部屋へやといい家具かぐといいインテリアインテリアといい、本当におしゃれだね。
    "Huang’s room is really stylish, both in terms of furniture and interior design."
  • このレストランレストランといいサービスサービスといい味あじといい、期待外れだった。
    "This restaurant was disappointing, both in service and in taste."

Comparison with Other Grammar Structures:

～といい…といい vs. ～なり…なり

～なり…なり is used to suggest options and has a slightly encouraging tone, whereas ～といい…といい is used to describe qualities and doesn't offer any choices.

  • ～といい…といい:
    "この映画は映像といい音楽といい、素晴らしい。"
    (Describes and admires the movie’s qualities.)
  • ～なり…なり:
    "おにぎりなりサンドイッチなり、何か買って食べてね。"
    (Suggests buying either an onigiri or a sandwich.)

Simpler Version

This grammar point is used to express admiration or criticism by highlighting two aspects. In everyday conversation, people might use simpler structures like ～と…と.

  • Original (～といい…といい):
    この映画えいがといい映像えいぞうといい音楽おんがくといい、最高の作品だ。
    "This movie is the best work, with both its beautiful visuals and excellent music."
  • Simpler Version:
    この映画えいがは映像えいぞうと音楽おんがくとが素晴らしくて、最高の作品だ。
    "This movie has wonderful visuals and music, making it the best work."

Nuance Difference: The original ～といい…といい emphasizes each aspect separately, often with an evaluative tone. The simpler version using ～と…と combines the aspects more seamlessly, sounding more natural in everyday speech.

Notes:

  • Used to express admiration or criticism by highlighting two aspects.
  • Has an evaluative tone, making it suitable for descriptive language.
  • Not typically used for neutral statements; conveys the speaker's opinion.

4. ～といわず…といわず

Meaning: "Both... and...; not just... but also..."; "From... to...; throughout..."

Japanese: 「～も…も区別なく全部・あらゆる所・いつも、同じようだ」

Usage: Conveys comprehensive inclusivity by listing examples that cover all parts, aspects, or times of a subject without exception.

Formality: Slightly literary. Used in both spoken and written Japanese, often in descriptive contexts.

Structure: 名詞 + といわず + 名詞 + といわず

Conjugation Examples:

  • 名詞 + といわず + 名詞 + といわず: 頭あたまといわず足あしといわず、泥だらけだった。
    "They were covered in mud from head to toe."

Example Sentences:

  • 砂浜すなはまで遊んでいた子供こどもたちは、手てといわず足あしといわず全身砂だらけだ。
    "The children playing on the beach were covered in sand, from their hands to their feet."
  • 室内で犬いぬを飼っているので、廊下ろうかといわず部屋へやといわず家中の毛が落ちている。
    "Since we have a dog indoors, there is fur everywhere, from the hallways to inside the rooms."
  • 営業マンえいぎょうまんの黄こうさんは平日へいじつといわず週末しゅうまつといわず、休む暇なく社外に出て働いている。
    "Huang, the salesperson, is out working tirelessly, whether it's weekdays or weekends."
  • オーエンオーエンの部屋へやは机つくえといわず床ゆかといわず、ゴミだらけだ。
    "Owen’s room is covered in garbage, from the desk to the floor."
  • 手てといわず顔かおといわず、泥だらけになって遊んでいた。
    "They were playing and got covered in mud, from their hands to their faces."

Comparison with Other Grammar Structures:

～といわず…といわず vs. ～なり…なり

～なり…なり is used to suggest options and encourage action, whereas ～といわず…といわず emphasizes that something affects every part or aspect without exception.

  • ～なり…なり:
    "おにぎりなりサンドイッチなり、何か買って食べてね。"
    (Suggests buying either an onigiri or a sandwich.)
  • ～といわず…といわず:
    "手といわず足といわず、泥だらけだった。"
    (Emphasizes being covered in mud from hands to feet.)

Simpler Version

This grammar point is a more formal and comprehensive way to express "both... and...". In everyday conversation, people might use simpler structures like ～も…も.

  • Original (～といわず…といわず):
    手てといわず顔かおといわず、泥だらけだった。
    "They were covered in mud from their hands to their faces."
  • Simpler Version:
    手ても顔かおも、泥だらけだった。
    "They were covered in mud on their hands and faces."

Nuance Difference: The original ～といわず…といわず emphasizes that mud covered every part without exception, giving a more comprehensive and dramatic description. The simpler version using ～も…も lists the areas without the same level of emphasis on inclusivity.

Notes:

  • Often used to describe undesirable situations.
  • Emphasizes that something affects every part or aspect.
  • Not typically used in positive contexts.
